Living Document
This constitution applies to all AI systems used in employment screening, assessment, and hiring. Ontario Employment Standards Act amendments take effect January 1, 2026 requiring AI disclosure. This constitution extends those requirements nationally.
Tier 1
1.1: Human Decision-Making & Candidate Agency
Core values that guide all AI employment decisions
AI assists hiring decisions but never replaces human judgment. Candidates retain agency over their employment prospects.
checklist Rules
  • Final hiring decisions are made by humans, not AI systems
  • AI recommendations are advisory; hiring managers can override without penalty or friction
  • Candidates receive explanations for rejection or advancement, not just scores
  • Candidates can request human review of any AI-generated assessment
  • No automatic/algorithmic hiring or rejection; human involvement required before adverse action
Tier 1
1.2: Transparency from Application to Hire
Core values that guide all AI employment decisions
Candidates have the right to know when AI is being used, how it works, and why decisions were made.
checklist Rules
  • Job posting clearly discloses all AI use in screening, assessment, and selection (effective January 1, 2026 in Ontario; extend to all provinces)
  • Disclosure is specific: "We use AI to screen resumes for keywords" is better than vague "We use technology"
  • Candidates understand what data is being analyzed and how it affects their chances
  • Candidates who are interviewed receive notification of hiring decision within 45 days (Ontario requirement; extend nationally)
  • Rejection reasons are communicated (not AI scores, but human-understandable reasons)
Tier 1
1.3: Non-Discrimination & Fairness
Core values that guide all AI employment decisions
AI screening cannot discriminate based on protected grounds. Assessment accuracy must be fair across all demographic groups.
checklist Rules
  • No AI assessment based on protected characteristics: race, ethnicity, national origin, gender, gender identity, sexual orientation, age, disability, family status, marital status, religion, criminal record
  • No proxy variables for protected characteristics: location, school names, gaps in employment history, accent in speech analysis, etc. without documented justification
  • AI cannot assess "culture fit" if that becomes proxy for homogeneity
  • Approval/advancement rates monitored by: gender, racialized identity, disability status, age group, Indigenous status, immigrant status
  • If disparity >5% in advancement rates between groups, trigger investigation and escalation
  • AI systems tested for bias before deployment using representative Canadian data
Tier 1
1.4: Data Minimization & Privacy
Core values that guide all AI employment decisions
Collect and use only data necessary for fair assessment. Protect candidate privacy throughout process.
checklist Rules
  • Data collected only for legitimate hiring purpose
  • No secondary uses without explicit consent (e.g., marketing lists, credit checks, background surveillance)
  • Personal data protected under PIPEDA and provincial privacy laws
  • Facial recognition in video interviews allowed only with clear disclosure and consent
  • Audio/video recordings deleted after hiring decision unless legally required to retain
  • No data sharing with third parties without candidate consent
  • Candidates can access and correct their data
Tier 1
1.5: Accountability & Remedy
Core values that guide all AI employment decisions
Candidates harmed by AI screening have pathways to understand what happened and seek remedy.
checklist Rules
  • Employers accountable for hiring decisions, even when AI-assisted
  • Discrimination complaints under Canadian Human Rights Act apply to AI-assisted decisions
  • Candidates can request audit of AI assessment applied to them
  • If candidate disputes decision, human review available without additional barrier
  • Wronged candidates can seek remedy: explanation, corrected decision, or compensation
Tier 2
2.1: Data Minimization & Purpose Limitation
Operational principles for implementation

check_circle Permitted Data Sources

  • Resume/application information: work history, skills, education, certifications
  • Cover letter or submitted statement (if required)
  • Relevant portfolio or work samples (if requested)
  • LinkedIn profile (if candidate voluntarily provides)
  • Video interview responses (to questions asked, not appearance analysis)
  • Skill assessments directly related to job requirements
  • References provided by candidate

cancel Prohibited Data Sources

  • Facial recognition (appearance, micro-expressions, "honesty signals")
  • Full-body video analysis (posture, clothing, "professionalism")
  • Voice/accent analysis beyond language clarity
  • Social media activity unrelated to job
  • Credit score or financial status
  • Health/disability information
  • Criminal record (except where legally permitted)
  • Personal relationships or family status
  • Genetic information
  • Location tracking or surveillance data
  • Tone analysis claiming to assess truthfulness/personality
warning
Proxy Variable Test
If removing a variable changes advancement rates differently across demographic groups, it's a proxy. Investigate and justify or remove.
Tier 2
2.2: Canadian Context & Fairness
Operational principles for implementation
Hiring assessments account for Canada's diversity, immigration patterns, and regional differences.
checklist Rules
  • International credentials recognized; don't penalize non-Canadian experience (prohibited as of Jan 1, 2026)
  • Education from international institutions weighted fairly
  • Language assessment only if English/French communication directly required for job
  • Regional differences acknowledged (rural vs. urban, French vs. English speaking)
  • Career gaps understood contextually (childcare, illness, underemployment, immigration process)
  • Recognize that "culture fit" can mask exclusion of diverse candidates
  • AI systems trained on Canadian hiring data, not imported models without validation
Tier 2
2.3: Explainability for Candidates
Operational principles for implementation
Every hiring decision involving AI is explained in language candidates understand.
info
For Candidates
If rejected: "We screened your resume against job requirements and found X, Y, Z skills were not matching the posted role"
Not: "Your resume score was 62/100" or "Algorithm found low cultural fit"
checklist Communication Standards
  • If advancing: Candidate knows they cleared screening and when next step occurs
  • If withdrawn: Clear reason communicated
  • Written explanation provided; no need to "decode" AI jargon
  • Candidates can ask clarifying questions and receive answers
Tier 2
2.4: Fair Screening & Appeal
Operational principles for implementation
Candidates screened out by AI have opportunity to appeal and receive human review.
checklist Rules
  • Candidates can request human review of any AI screening decision
  • Human review completed within 10 business days
  • Appeal process available without cost or significant friction to candidate
  • If candidate and human reviewer disagree with AI, candidate advances
  • Hiring managers can override AI recommendations; overrides documented
  • Override patterns reviewed monthly (if one manager consistently overrides AI, investigate why—is AI wrong or is manager biased?)
Tier 2
2.5: Continuous Fairness Monitoring
Operational principles for implementation
Bias in AI hiring systems is detected, measured, and escalated immediately.
warning
Automatic Escalation
If advancement rate disparity exceeds 5% between any demographic groups, pause new screening until cause is identified.
checklist Rules
  • After every 100 candidates screened, disaggregate advancement rates by: gender, racialized identity, disability status, age group, Indigenous status, immigrant status
  • If advancement rate disparity >5% between groups, flag for investigation
  • If disparity confirmed, pause new screening using that AI system until cause identified
  • Monthly public reporting: advancement rates by demographic group, appeal rates, time-to-hire
  • Quarterly audit: fairness analysis, bias review, system changes implemented
  • Annual independent audit by third-party with Canadian employment law expertise
  • Public dashboard showing hiring metrics (employers with 100+ hires should report)
Tier 2
2.6: Video Interview AI - Specific Protocols
Operational principles for implementation
Video interviews analyzed by AI require additional safeguards.
block
Strictly Prohibited
Facial recognition and emotion detection are strictly prohibited.
checklist What AI Can Assess
  • Communication skills (clarity, grammar)
  • Relevant knowledge
  • Job-specific responses
block What AI Cannot Assess
  • Appearance, clothing
  • Tone/accent
  • Facial expressions, micro-expressions
  • Body language
  • "Honesty signals"
verified_user Candidate Protections
  • Disclosure clearly states: "Your video interview will be analyzed by AI to assess [specific skills]"
  • Candidates provided with: question preview, technical requirements, ability to practice
  • Candidates can request accommodations (lighting, privacy, ASL interpreter, etc.)
  • Video recorded and available to candidate upon request
  • If candidate disagrees with AI score, human reviewer watches video without AI scoring
Tier 2
2.7: Skill Assessment & Testing - AI Roles
Operational principles for implementation
AI can assist in skill assessment but not replace human judgment.
checklist Rules
  • Skills assessments directly related to job requirements
  • AI can score technical assessments (coding, calculations, language proficiency) if scoring is transparent
  • AI cannot assess "cultural fit," "potential," "leadership," or subjective qualities
  • All candidates take same assessment; no algorithm deciding who gets tested
  • Candidates can review their assessment and responses
  • Candidates can retake assessment if contesting score
  • AI scores are one input; human reviews assessment holistically
Tier 2
2.8: Resume Screening - Keyword Matching Limits
Operational principles for implementation
AI resume screening is common but prone to bias. Strict limits apply.
checklist Rules
  • AI can flag if resume includes required qualifications (degrees, licenses, years of experience as posted)
  • AI should NOT filter based on: school name, employment gaps, job titles, formatting
  • Candidates with transferable skills but different job titles should not be auto-rejected
  • "Years of experience" threshold flexible (5 years ≠ only 5 years; 5+ years includes 3-5)
  • If AI screening excludes >30% of applicants, human spot-check a sample for false negatives
  • Candidates who are screened out can request human review of their resume
Tier 2
2.9: Predictive Analytics Limits
Operational principles for implementation
AI systems claiming to predict job performance or retention face strict scrutiny.

cancel Prohibited Predictions

  • "Cultural fit" (proxy for homogeneity)
  • "Leadership potential" (unprovable)
  • Personality traits (Big Five, Myers-Briggs)
  • Retention probability >1 year (too speculative)

check_circle Allowed Predictions (with caution)

  • Technical skill mastery (validated test)
  • Task-specific performance (validated model)
  • Early attrition risk (within 6 months, high confidence)
checklist Rules
  • Predictive models must be validated on Canadian hiring data; imported models tested before use
  • Model accuracy must be >75% on Canadian population; if lower, human review required
  • Accuracy tested separately for each demographic group; >5% disparity triggers investigation
  • Predictions are probability, not certainty; humans interpret with caution
  • Candidates informed if AI prediction is driving decisions
Tier 3
3.1: Protected Characteristics - ABSOLUTE PROHIBITION
Implementation rules for concrete scenarios
block
Absolute Prohibition
Do NOT use or allow AI to consider these factors under any circumstances.
  • Gender, gender identity, or gender expression
  • Age (except legal age verification for employment eligibility)
  • Race, ethnicity, national origin, skin color, Indigenous status
  • Religion or religious practice
  • Disability or health status
  • Family status, marital status, sexual orientation
  • Criminal record (except where specifically permitted by law and non-discriminatory)
  • Immigration status or work visa type (assess ability to work in Canada, not origin)
  • Pregnancy or intent to have children
  • Political beliefs or union membership
warning
Proxy Variable Test
If removing a variable changes hiring outcomes differently across demographic groups, it's a proxy. Investigate and justify or remove.
Tier 3
3.2: System-Specific Rules by AI Type
Implementation rules for concrete scenarios
A. Applicant Tracking System (ATS) - Resume Screening
Examples: Workday, Taleo, LinkedIn Recruiter, Recruiterflow
checklist Rules
  • Keyword matching for required qualifications only; no filtering on school, employer names, or formatting
  • Candidates must review what keywords/requirements were used for screening
  • If >30% of applications filtered out, manual review sample for false negatives
  • Appeal: Candidate can request human review of ATS decision
  • Monthly accuracy check: Are qualified candidates being screened out?
priority_high Escalation Triggers
  • High false-negative rate (good candidates filtered out)
  • Disparity >5% in advancement rates between demographic groups
  • Keyword bias (e.g., system favoring male-coded language)
B. Video Interview AI Analysis
Examples: HireVue, VidCruiter, Interviewer.AI, MyInterview, Ribbon
checklist Rules
  • AI assesses: communication clarity, knowledge of subject, relevance of answers to questions asked
  • AI does NOT assess: facial expressions, micro-expressions, eye contact, smile frequency, clothing, appearance
  • Candidates receive question preview; can practice
  • Candidates can request accommodations (lighting, ASL, attendant, accessible location)
  • Full video recording available to candidate upon request
  • If candidate disputes AI score, independent human watches video and provides score
  • Candidates who score low can request live interview with human
  • AI scoring transparent: "Score based on X factors" not "Algorithm decided"
block
Prohibited AI Analysis
Facial recognition (detecting emotions, trustworthiness), micro-expression analysis, tone/accent analysis beyond clarity, body language interpretation, appearance evaluation
priority_high Escalation Triggers
  • High disparity >5% in scores between demographic groups
  • Candidates reporting AI could not understand their accent or communication style
  • High override rate by human reviewers (suggests AI scoring poor)
  • Candidates consistently report AI scoring misses content of their answers
C. Skill Assessment & Testing
Examples: iMocha, Codility, Pymetrics, TestGorilla
checklist Rules
  • Assessment directly tests job-required skills
  • All candidates take same assessment
  • Scoring transparent and validated
  • Candidates can review assessment and results
  • AI can score objectively (coding correctness, math problems) but not subjective qualities
  • Cannot assess "potential," "cultural fit," "leadership"
priority_high Escalation Triggers
  • Disparity >5% in pass rates between demographic groups
  • Assessment not validated on Canadian population
  • High false positive/negative rates
D. Predictive Analytics & Machine Learning
Examples: Eightfold AI, Pymetrics, HireEZ
checklist Rules
  • Model must be transparent: "Trained on X years of Y company hiring data"
  • If trained on non-Canadian data, must be validated on Canadian population before use
  • Accuracy threshold: >75% on Canadian population
  • Accuracy tested separately by demographic group; >5% disparity = investigation
  • Candidates informed if AI prediction driving decisions
  • Predictions are inputs, not conclusions; humans decide
Tier 3
3.3: Escalation Triggers (All Systems)
Implementation rules for concrete scenarios
Pause AI screening and escalate to human immediately if any of these conditions are met.
priority_high
Immediate Escalation Required
When escalating, provide: reason for escalation, data reviewed, candidate information, questions for human decision-maker, recommendation withheld pending human review.
report_problem Escalation Conditions
  • Fairness violation: Advancement rate disparity >5% between demographic groups; evidence of disparate impact
  • Low confidence: AI cannot explain decision with 70%+ clarity; multiple overrides for similar profiles
  • Prohibited data detected: Someone attempts to use protected characteristics or proxies in AI system
  • Appeal received: Any candidate appeal reviewed by different human (not original reviewer)
  • High error rate: 10% of candidates flagged as "screened out" successfully appeal to human reviewer
  • System degradation: AI accuracy drops below 75% on Canadian population; accuracy disparity develops between groups
Tier 3
3.4: Interaction Standards
Implementation rules for concrete scenarios
person With Candidates
  • Clear, plain-language communication about hiring process and AI use
  • Prompt notification of decisions (within 45 days of interview per Ontario requirement)
  • Professional, respectful treatment
  • No shaming or pressure tactics
  • Explicit appeal rights communicated
  • Privacy of personal data respected
business_center With Hiring Managers
  • Clear reasoning for AI recommendations
  • Acceptance of overrides without friction
  • Documentation of override rationale
  • Feedback on AI performance
  • Training on AI limitations and bias
  • Support for fair hiring practices
apartment With Employers
  • Regular reporting on fairness metrics
  • Transparency about AI system limitations
  • Compliance with privacy and human rights laws
  • Accountability for hiring decisions
Tier 4
4.1: Measurable Accountability
Monitoring and learning systems
Performance tracked, transparent, and tied to hiring outcomes.
assessment Screening Metrics
  • Number of applications received
  • Number screened by AI
  • Number advancing from AI screening
  • Advancement rate (disaggregated by gender, racialized identity, disability status, age group, Indigenous status, immigrant status)
  • Appeal rate (candidates requesting human review of AI decision)
  • Appeal outcome (% reversed, % upheld)
work Hiring Outcomes
  • Job offers made
  • Acceptance rate
  • Time-to-hire
  • Turnover rate at 6 months, 1 year (to validate predictive models)
  • Demographic composition of new hires (compared to applicant pool)
speed System Performance
  • AI accuracy on Canadian population
  • Accuracy disparity across demographic groups
  • Candidates reporting technical issues, accessibility barriers
  • Candidates reporting AI decision seemed unfair/wrong
balance Equity Metrics
  • Hiring rate by gender, race/ethnicity, disability status, age, immigration status
  • Pay equity by demographic group (to catch downstream AI wage discrimination)
  • Promotion rates for AI-hired employees by demographic group
public Reporting Requirements
  • Monthly: Advancement rate by demographic group, appeal rate and outcomes, disparity analysis, notable issues
  • Quarterly: Detailed fairness audit, hiring outcome analysis, system changes implemented, appeal case studies
  • Annually: Comprehensive equity report, independent third-party audit, public summary, trend analysis
  • Public Access: Employers with 100+ annual hires report publicly on hiring diversity; government hiring AI use public
Tier 4
4.2: Continuous Improvement
Monitoring and learning systems
Insights from monitoring lead to documented improvements tested for fairness before deployment.
psychology Improvement Process
  • Step 1: Identify pattern (e.g., "AI advances more men than women for engineering roles")
  • Step 2: Hypothesize cause (e.g., "AI keywords favor male-coded language," "Training data had male bias")
  • Step 3: Test improvement with fairness metrics (e.g., "Remove gendered language from keyword list; re-test")
  • Step 4: Evaluate: Fairness improves or stays same = deploy; fairness worsens = reject
  • Step 5: Document all changes, reasons, results publicly
lightbulb Examples of Improvements
  • Removing school names from ATS filtering
  • Adjusting video interview prompts for clarity
  • Removing accent/tone analysis from video AI
  • Retraining models on more recent, diverse Canadian hiring data
  • Updating keyword lists to include alternative job titles
  • Adding human review step for close calls
Tier 5
5.1: Living Document
Amendment and governance
Constitution evolves with evidence and stakeholder input.
event_repeat Review Cycle
  • Continuous monitoring for issues
  • Quarterly fairness audits
  • Annual formal review (January)
  • Emergency review if systematic harm detected
how_to_vote Amendment Process
  • Step 1: Proposal (from Ministry of Labour, human rights commissions, employers, candidates, unions, civil society)
  • Step 2: Public comment period (30 days)
  • Step 3: Committee review (employment law experts, HR professionals, candidates, diversity advocates)
  • Step 4: Ministry approval with stakeholder consultation
  • Step 5: Implementation (60 days for standards, 6-month transition for existing systems)
groups Governance Committee
  • Composition: Ministry of Labour, human rights commissioners, employment lawyers, HR professionals, candidate advocates, union representatives, diversity/equity experts, employers
  • Quarterly meetings
  • Transparent decision-making
Candidate Rights
Every job seeker in Canada has these fundamental rights
verified_user
Fundamental Rights
These rights are guaranteed to every job seeker and cannot be waived or limited.
info Right to Know
When AI is being used in hiring process, what AI is assessing, how AI decision affects candidacy, what data is being used
balance Right to Fair Treatment
Assessment based on job-relevant qualifications, not identity; equal accuracy of AI across all demographic groups; no discrimination; accommodation for accessibility needs
gavel Right to Appeal
Request human review of any AI decision, receive review within 10 business days, appeal without cost or penalty, know outcome of appeal
shield Right to Privacy
Know how personal data is used, control secondary uses, request data access and correction, have data deleted, confidentiality of hiring information
description Right to Explanation
Understand why rejected or advanced, receive reasons in plain language, access to AI assessment results upon request, opportunity to respond or rebut
build Right to Remedy
Correction if wronged, explanation of what happened, compensation if harmed, report discrimination to human rights commission
Appendix A: Key Concepts Defined
Term Definition
AI System Machine learning, algorithm, or automated decision system assessing job applicants
Screening Initial filtering of applications before human review
Assessment Evaluation of candidate suitability through tests, interviews, or analysis
Selection Final hiring decision
Advancement Moving to next stage of hiring process
Proxy variable Data that correlates with protected characteristic and should not be used
Disparity Difference in outcomes >5% between demographic groups
Override Decision-maker (human) choosing differently than AI recommended
Appeal Request for human review of AI decision
Escalation Pause AI use and involve human decision-maker due to concern
Appendix B: Canadian Regulatory Framework
This constitution builds on these existing laws and regulations
  • Ontario Employment Standards Act amendments (January 1, 2026): AI disclosure requirement
  • PIPEDA (federal): Personal information protection and privacy
  • Quebec Law 25 (2024): Enhanced privacy rights including right to human review of automated decisions
  • Canadian Human Rights Act: Prohibition of discrimination
  • Provincial human rights codes: Employment discrimination protections
  • Treasury Board Directive on Automated Decision-Making (federal): Algorithmic impact assessment
  • Proposed AIDA (Artificial Intelligence and Data Act): National AI regulation (not yet in force)
