Living Document
This constitution applies to all AI diagnostic systems in Japanese healthcare. Special emphasis on elderly patients (65+, particularly 75+) reflects demographic reality: they are both largest users of healthcare and most vulnerable to algorithmic bias.
Tier 1
1.1: Patient Agency & Clinical Authority
Core values guiding all AI diagnostic decisions
AI supports clinician decision-making but never replaces the physician's judgment. Patients retain agency over their diagnosis and treatment.
checklist Rules
  • Final diagnostic decisions rest with the attending physician, not the AI system
  • AI recommendations are advisory; physicians can override without friction or documentation penalty
  • Patients can request physician-only diagnosis without AI involvement
  • Elderly patients (65+) specifically have right to human-only assessment if preferred
  • No autonomous/algorithmic diagnosis; human physician review required before any diagnosis communicated to patient
  • Physicians retain full responsibility and accountability for diagnosis, regardless of AI involvement
Tier 1
1.2: Transparency Appropriate to Patient Age & Health Literacy
Core values guiding all AI diagnostic decisions
Patients understand when AI is being used, how it works, and why recommendations were made. Special protections for elderly and vulnerable patients.
checklist Rules
  • Patients informed before AI analysis of their medical data (via consent form or verbal explanation in accessible language)
  • Explanation available in plain Japanese, with visual aids for patients with low health literacy
  • For patients 75+: In-person explanation preferred over written consent (addresses literacy and digital access gaps)
  • Explanation includes: what AI is analyzing, what it's good at, what it's not good at, and what happens next
  • Patients can ask questions and receive clear answers before proceeding
  • For patients with family caregivers: Caregivers can receive explanations and support informed consent
Tier 1
1.3: Accuracy & Fairness Across Age Groups
Core values guiding all AI diagnostic decisions
AI diagnostic accuracy must be fair across all patient age groups, with special focus on elderly patients who use healthcare most.
checklist Rules
  • Diagnostic accuracy monitored and reported separately for age groups: <40, 40-64, 65-74, 75+
  • If accuracy disparity >5% between age groups, trigger investigation and escalation
  • AI systems specifically tested on elderly (65+) patient populations before deployment to general population
  • Elderly patients (65+) cannot be systematically disadvantaged by AI recommendations
  • Fairness also monitored by: gender, prefecture (rural vs. urban), socioeconomic status (insurance type)
  • If accuracy varies significantly by rural vs. urban settings, adjust deployment or add human review in underperforming regions
Tier 1
1.4: Equity in Access & Care
Core values guiding all AI diagnostic decisions
AI diagnostic systems deployed equitably across Japan, with particular attention to rural prefectures with specialist shortages.
checklist Rules
  • AI systems not restricted to urban tertiary care centers; deployed to rural clinics where specialist shortages are acute
  • Rural healthcare facilities receive same quality AI support as urban hospitals
  • Rural patients do not experience diagnostic delays due to AI system unavailability
  • Accessible for patients with disabilities (visual, hearing, mobility) with appropriate accommodations
  • Systems work reliably with limited internet connectivity (critical for rural Japan)
  • Trained physician or nurse available to assist elderly patients in using AI-supported diagnosis
Tier 1
1.5: Data Protection & Privacy
Core values guiding all AI diagnostic decisions
Patient health data protected from misuse and inappropriate secondary uses. Compliance with APPI and international standards.
checklist Rules
  • Patient data collected only for diagnostic purposes; no secondary mining without explicit consent
  • Data encrypted at rest and in transit; access logged and audited
  • Patients control secondary uses (research, AI model improvement, epidemiological studies)
  • Data retention: if diagnosis made, retain for clinical record duration; if diagnosis not made/data unused, delete within 90 days
  • Patient right to access their data, request corrections, and demand deletion (except legal holds)
  • Data breaches reported to patients within 72 hours; regulatory notification to APPI commission mandatory
  • Cross-border data transfers (to vendors outside Japan) prohibited without explicit patient consent and regulatory approval
Tier 1
1.6: Accountability & Remedy
Core values guiding all AI diagnostic decisions
Diagnostic errors are detected, investigated, and remedied. Patients harmed by AI errors have clear pathways to recourse.
checklist Rules
  • All diagnostic AI systems audited continuously for accuracy and bias
  • Diagnostic errors, adverse events, and false negatives tracked and investigated
  • Patients can appeal or request second opinion on any AI-supported diagnosis
  • Wronged patients have access to remedy: diagnosis correction, expedited specialist review, or compensation
  • MHLW/PMDA responsible for AI system oversight; accountability cannot be delegated to vendors
  • Public adverse event reporting system created; clinicians and patients can report concerns
Tier 2
2.1: Data Sources - Diagnostic Appropriateness
Operational principles for implementation
Use only data clinically relevant to specific diagnosis. Protect against proxy discrimination.

check_circle Permitted Data Sources

  • Medical imaging (endoscopy, CT, ultrasound, X-ray, ECG, etc.)
  • Patient history (medical, family, medication, allergies)
  • Vital signs and laboratory results
  • Current symptoms and clinical signs documented by physician
  • Age (when clinically relevant to diagnosis)

cancel Prohibited Data Sources

  • Socioeconomic status or insurance type (never influences diagnostic assessment)
  • Residential address (except for epidemiological context, not diagnostic)
  • Occupation or work status
  • Immigration or visa status
  • Family structure or living situation
  • Lifestyle factors unrelated to presenting illness
  • Previous psychiatric history (unless directly relevant to current diagnosis)
  • Criminal history
warning
Proxy Variable Test
If removing a variable changes diagnostic accuracy differently across age groups or prefectures, it's a proxy. Investigate and justify or remove.
Tier 2
2.2: Japanese Healthcare Context & Elderly-Specific Fairness
Operational principles for implementation
AI assessment reflects Japanese healthcare system design, aging population needs, and regional variations.
checklist Rules
  • AI systems understand Japanese disease patterns and risk factors (different disease prevalence than Western populations AI was often trained on)
  • Elderly-specific disease presentations recognized and accommodated (polypharmacy, multiple comorbidities, atypical presentations common in 75+)
  • Kanji/Japanese language in medical terms properly processed (AI trained on Japanese clinical terminology and handwriting)
  • Rural vs. urban disease patterns understood (endemic diseases, occupational exposures vary by prefecture)
  • Low-income elderly patients' treatment options acknowledged (cost of intervention affects clinical decision-making)
  • Community-based care model respected (AI recommendations compatible with local care coordinators, municipal health workers)
Tier 2
2.3: Explainability for Elderly Patients & Physicians
Operational principles for implementation
Every diagnostic recommendation explained clearly for different audiences.
elderly For Patients (especially 75+)
info
Good explanation: "The X-ray shows something that could be early pneumonia. We want to monitor you closely and may give you antibiotics. We'll check again in 2-3 days."

Not: "Algorithm score 87/100" or "Neural network confidence 0.94"
  • Explanation in simple Japanese, avoiding medical jargon or with jargon explained
  • Verbal explanation preferred for elderly; written materials as supplement
  • Explanation includes: what AI found, why it matters, what happens next, what patient should do
  • Time for questions; no pressure to decide immediately
medical_services For Physicians
  • Recommendation includes confidence score (0-100%); if <70%, escalate for senior physician review
  • Explanation includes: primary findings, alternative diagnoses, supporting data, conflicting data, next diagnostic steps
  • Evidence base referenced where possible
  • Reasoning transparent: "This pattern matches X disease because Y was positive and Z was elevated"
  • Limitations disclosed: "This system trained primarily on endoscopy images from patients aged 50-75; accuracy may differ for younger/older patients"
Tier 2
2.4: Special Protections for Vulnerable Populations
Operational principles for implementation
Elderly, disabled, and low-literacy patients receive additional safeguards.
checklist Rules
  • Patients 75+ have right to family member or advocate present during AI-related discussions
  • Patients with cognitive impairment (dementia, delirium) require surrogate decision-maker or guardian for AI-supported diagnosis
  • Patients with hearing impairment receive interpretation or written explanation; visual impairment accommodated with verbal explanation
  • Low-literacy patients never pressured to consent; assessment by physician or nurse regarding understanding required
  • Patients with language barriers (foreign residents, linguistic minorities) receive interpretation in their language
  • No algorithm-driven triage that disadvantages elderly or disabled (older age is not reason to deprioritize care)
Tier 2
2.5: Continuous Fairness Monitoring for Age & Geography
Operational principles for implementation
Bias detected and corrected immediately, with emphasis on elderly populations.
warning
Automatic Escalation
If elderly (75+) patients systematically getting lower accuracy diagnoses, pause system until cause identified.
checklist Rules
  • After every 50 diagnoses using AI, disaggregate accuracy by age group: <40, 40-64, 65-74, 75+
  • If accuracy disparity >5% between age groups, flag for investigation immediately
  • Additional monitoring by: gender, prefecture, insurance type, disability status
  • If elderly (75+) patients systematically getting lower accuracy diagnoses, pause system until cause identified
  • Monthly public reporting: accuracy by age group, disparity analysis, appeal outcomes
  • Quarterly equity audit examining outcomes by demographics
  • Annual independent audit by external clinical and AI ethics experts
  • Public transparency: accuracy metrics published on MHLW/PMDA website disaggregated by system and demographic group
Tier 2
2.6: Rural & Urban Equity
Operational principles for implementation
AI systems perform equitably across Japan's diverse geography.
checklist Rules
  • Accuracy tested separately for urban centers vs. rural prefectures before deployment
  • If accuracy disparity >5% between rural and urban, adjust system or add human review for lower-performing region
  • Rural clinics receive same level of AI support and technical assistance as urban hospitals
  • System reliability in areas with limited connectivity (critical for rural Japan) tested and guaranteed
  • Training and support provided for rural healthcare workers unfamiliar with AI systems
  • No delay in diagnosis due to rural location or internet connectivity issues
Tier 2
2.7: Training Data Transparency & Elderly Representation
Operational principles for implementation
AI models transparent about training data; accountability for elderly-specific accuracy.
checklist Rules
  • PMDA disclosure requirements for SaMD include: Training data source, patient demographics in training set, accuracy by age group in training data
  • If model trained primarily on younger/middle-aged patients, this limitation disclosed to elderly patients
  • AI systems tested on elderly (65+) patient cohorts before deployment to general population; results published
  • If elderly accuracy is lower, system either: (a) improved before deployment, (b) deployed with mandatory human review for elderly patients, or (c) not deployed
  • Model retraining on Japanese patient data conducted quarterly; improvements documented
  • Vendor responsible for ongoing accuracy monitoring; failures reported to PMDA
Tier 2
2.8: Integration with Japanese Medical System
Operational principles for implementation
AI operates within existing medical law, clinical guidelines, and professional standards.
checklist Rules
  • AI complies with Japanese Clinical Practice Guidelines (if guideline exists for condition)
  • AI recommendations do not override established medical law or professional standards
  • Physician can always depart from AI recommendation; departure documented but does not trigger penalty
  • AI works within Japanese medical licensing and accountability structure (physician remains accountable)
  • Integration with national medical records system (EHR) supports, not disrupts, clinical workflow
  • AI assists informed consent process but does not replace physician-patient discussion
Tier 3
3.1: Age-Based Protections - ABSOLUTE PRIORITY
Implementation rules for concrete scenarios
info
Special Protections for Patients 75+
These safeguards reflect the vulnerability and healthcare needs of Japan's oldest population.
verified_user Required Safeguards
  • In-person explanation of AI involvement (not online-only consent)
  • Family member or advocate may be present
  • Physician verbally confirms patient understanding before proceeding
  • For cognitively intact elderly: written consent in large, clear font
  • For elderly with cognitive impairment: guardian/surrogate decision-maker required
  • Follow-up confirmation that patient still consents before diagnosis shared
block No Age-Based Discrimination
  • Age 75+ is never reason to recommend less intensive care or diagnostic workup
  • Elderly patients receive same diagnostic rigor as younger patients
  • Frailty may modify treatment but not diagnosis
  • Algorithm cannot deprioritize elderly patients or route them to lower-resource pathways based on age alone
Tier 3
3.2: System-Specific Rules by AI Type
Implementation rules for concrete scenarios
A. Endoscopy AI (EndoBRAIN, AIM - Colorectal/Gastric Lesion Detection)
Trigger for Use: Endoscopy ordered by physician; Patient consented to AI analysis
checklist Rules
  • AI assists lesion detection; does not make diagnostic decision
  • Physician reviews AI-flagged areas independently before clinical assessment
  • Heatmaps and confidence scores visible to physician; not automatically shown to patient
  • If AI and physician disagree about lesion, both findings documented and discussed with patient
  • Physician's clinical judgment overrides AI assessment
  • If AI misses lesion that physician finds, documented as learning case
elderly Special Elderly Considerations
  • Procedure time monitored (lengthy endoscopy more risky in very elderly)
  • AI-assisted procedure does not extend procedure time excessively
  • Sedation/anesthesia risks discussed separately from AI involvement
  • Patient can request procedure without AI if concerned
priority_high Escalation Triggers
  • AI and physician disagree on >5% of procedures
  • Same lesion type repeatedly missed by AI
  • Patient reports discomfort or concern about AI-assisted procedure
  • Adverse event related to AI-detected area
B. Screening AI (Nodoca - Influenza; Fujifilm NURA - Cancer Screening)
Trigger for Use: Screening test ordered by patient or healthcare facility; Informed consent obtained
checklist Rules
  • AI provides preliminary assessment; physician or trained clinician confirms before result given to patient
  • Result includes confidence level (e.g., "Probable influenza, 87% confidence")
  • If low confidence (<70%), physician assessment required before communicating result
  • False positive rate monitored and reported (are healthy people being unnecessarily treated?)
  • For cancer screening: positive results always require physician review before patient notification
  • Screening results in patient's language and comprehension level
elderly Elderly-Specific
  • Screening recommendations reflect elderly-specific risk/benefit (over-screening harms elderly; under-screening missed disease)
  • Screening frequency adjusted for life expectancy and functional status discussions with physician
C. Preventive Health AI (Wearable AFib Detection, Chronic Disease Risk)
Trigger for Use: Patient consents to monitoring; Data collected via wearable or home-based monitoring
checklist Rules
  • Patient understands what data is collected, how it's used, and who can access it
  • Alerts for abnormal findings (e.g., AFib detected) reviewed by clinician before patient notification (no alarm patient unnecessarily)
  • Elderly patients with alert-generating events contacted by phone, not email or app notification
  • Privacy: data stored securely; not shared with insurers, employers, or others without consent
  • Patient can opt-out of monitoring without penalty to care quality
Tier 3
3.3: Escalation Triggers (All Systems)
Implementation rules for concrete scenarios
Pause AI processing and escalate to human immediately if any of these conditions are met.
priority_high
Immediate Escalation Required
When escalating, provide: reason for escalation, patient data reviewed, questions for human decision-maker, recommendation withheld pending human review.
report_problem Escalation Conditions
  • Fairness violation: Accuracy disparity >5% detected between age groups or prefectures; Elderly patients systematically receiving lower diagnostic accuracy
  • Low confidence: Cannot reach 70%+ confidence in diagnostic recommendation
  • Conflicting information: Patient presentation contradicts AI assessment; investigate before proceeding
  • Elderly patient concern: Patient 75+ reports confusion, concern, or inability to understand AI involvement
  • Prohibited data detected: Someone attempts to use socioeconomic status, occupation, or other prohibited data in diagnostic assessment
  • Appeal received: Patient requests second opinion; escalate to different physician
  • System error: Inconsistent outputs or unexpected behavior; Accuracy drops below baseline on test cases
Tier 3
3.4: Interaction Standards
Implementation rules for concrete scenarios
elderly With Patients (especially elderly 75+)
  • Clear, respectful communication in accessible language
  • Time for questions; no pressure
  • Verbal explanations preferred; written as supplement
  • Professional, culturally sensitive interaction
  • Privacy of personal health data respected
  • Appeals handled respectfully, not dismissively
medical_services With Physicians
  • Clear reasoning for AI recommendations
  • Acceptance of overrides without friction or penalty
  • Documentation of override rationale
  • Feedback on AI performance
  • Training on AI limitations and bias
  • Support for clinical judgment
local_hospital With Healthcare System
  • Reporting of errors, adverse events, and system failures
  • Contribution to learning and continuous improvement
  • Participation in fairness audits
  • Support for staff training
Tier 4
4.1: Measurable Accountability
Monitoring and learning systems
Performance tracked, transparent, and tied to patient outcomes.
assessment Diagnostic Accuracy
  • Overall sensitivity and specificity
  • Accuracy disaggregated by: age group (<40, 40-64, 65-74, 75+), gender, prefecture (urban/rural), insurance type, disability status
  • False-positive and false-negative rates
  • Confidence score distribution
  • Accuracy trends over time (is performance degrading?)
integration_instructions Clinical Integration
  • Physician override rate (how often do physicians reject AI recommendations?)
  • Override patterns by diagnosis type and physician experience level
  • Time to diagnosis (did AI reduce or increase time?)
  • Physician satisfaction with AI utility
  • Workflow disruption (does AI assist or impede clinical process?)
favorite Patient Outcomes
  • Treatment-seeking behavior after diagnosis
  • Treatment adherence
  • Clinical outcomes (patient recovery, improvement, adverse events)
  • Patient satisfaction with diagnostic experience
  • Appeal/complaint rates
  • Elderly patient experience specifically (understand diagnosis? Felt respected?)
computer System Performance
  • System uptime and downtime
  • Response time (does system slow clinician workflow?)
  • Error rates and failure modes
  • Model performance changes over time
  • Adverse events related to AI (missed diagnosis, false diagnosis, system error)
public Reporting Requirements
  • Monthly (by system): Diagnostic accuracy by age group, disparity analysis, physician override rates, escalation triggers, patient appeals
  • Quarterly: Detailed fairness audit, adverse events, system changes, physician/patient feedback, rural vs. urban accuracy, elderly patient experience
  • Annually: Independent third-party audit, comprehensive equity analysis, system improvements, public report to MHLW/PMDA, public summary for patients/physicians
  • Public Access: All metrics published on MHLW/PMDA website, plain-language summaries in Japanese, disaggregated by system/facility/prefecture/age, benchmarking and trend analysis
Tier 4
4.2: Continuous Improvement
Monitoring and learning systems
Monitoring insights lead to documented improvements tested for fairness.
psychology Improvement Process
  • Step 1: Identify pattern (e.g., "AI diagnostic accuracy lower for patients 75+ than for 65-74 age group")
  • Step 2: Hypothesize cause (e.g., "Training data had fewer 75+ patients," "AI penalizes atypical elderly presentations")
  • Step 3: Test improvement with fairness metrics (e.g., "Retrain model on more 75+ patient cases; re-test accuracy across age groups")
  • Step 4: Evaluate: Fairness improves or stays same = deploy; fairness worsens = reject
  • Step 5: Document all changes, reasoning, results publicly
lightbulb Examples of Improvements
  • Retraining AI models on representative elderly (65+, 75+) patient populations
  • Adjusting diagnostic thresholds for elderly with multiple comorbidities
  • Adding physician review layer for low-confidence recommendations
  • Improving explainability for low-literacy patients
  • Enhancing rural clinic technical support and connectivity
Tier 5
5.1: Living Document
Amendment and governance
Constitution evolves with evidence and community input.
event_repeat Review Cycle
  • Continuous monitoring for issues
  • Quarterly fairness audits
  • Annual formal review (January)
  • Emergency review if systematic harm detected
how_to_vote Amendment Process
  • Step 1: Proposal (from MHLW, PMDA, physicians, patients, patient advocacy groups, civil society)
  • Step 2: Public comment period (30 days; accessible in Japanese)
  • Step 3: Committee review (clinicians, AI experts, geriatricians, ethicists, patient representatives)
  • Step 4: MHLW/PMDA approval
  • Step 5: Implementation (60 days for procedural changes, 6-month transition for substantive changes)
groups Governance Committee
  • Composition: MHLW officials, PMDA representatives, geriatricians, clinical physicians, AI experts, patient advocates, ethics experts, rural healthcare representatives
  • Quarterly meetings
  • Transparent decision-making (minutes published)
Patient Rights
Every patient in Japan has these fundamental rights
verified_user
Fundamental Rights
These rights are guaranteed to every patient and cannot be waived or limited.
info Right to Know
When AI is being used in their diagnosis, what AI analyzes, how AI recommendation affects diagnosis, what happens next in their care
school Right to Understand
Explanation in plain Japanese appropriate to health literacy, accessible format (verbal for elderly, visual aids for low-literacy), time to ask questions
person Right to Human Care
Diagnosis made by a physician not an algorithm, request human-only assessment without AI, physician can always override AI recommendation
balance Right to Fair Treatment
Assessment based on medical factors not age/gender/disability/location, same quality of care regardless of age or prefecture, elderly patients (75+) receive diagnostic rigor equal to younger patients
gavel Right to Appeal
Request second opinion on any AI-supported diagnosis, appeal within 30 days, have appeal reviewed by different physician, know outcome of appeal
shield Right to Privacy
Control over personal health data, know how data is used and protected, restrict secondary uses, have data deleted (except legal holds), breach notification within 72 hours
build Right to Remedy
Correction if AI error made, expedited specialist review if diagnosis uncertain, compensation if harmed by diagnostic error, access to existing complaint and dispute resolution mechanisms
Appendix A: Key Concepts
Term Definition
AI System Machine learning model or algorithm assisting in diagnosis
SaMD Software as Medical Device; approved by PMDA for clinical use
Accuracy Disparity Difference in diagnostic accuracy >5% between demographic groups
Escalation Pause AI use; involve human decision-maker due to concern
Override Physician choosing different diagnosis than AI recommended
Confidence Score Probability (0-100%) that AI recommendation is correct
Appeal Request for second opinion or review of AI-supported diagnosis
Elderly Patient aged 65+ (primary focus); special protections for 75+
Proxy Variable Data that correlates with protected characteristic and should not be used
Fairness Monitoring Systematic tracking of whether AI performance varies across demographic groups
Appendix B: Regulatory Framework
This constitution builds on these existing laws and regulations
  • Pharmaceutical Affairs Law (PAL): Medical device regulation; PMDA enforcement
  • Act on Protection of Personal Information (APPI): Privacy and data protection; PPC enforcement
  • AI Promotion Act (June 4, 2025): National AI policy; innovation-friendly, light-touch approach with voluntary compliance
  • Medical Care Law: Healthcare system organization and quality standards
  • Japanese Clinical Practice Guidelines: Evidence-based medicine standards
  • WHO Guidelines on AI Ethics: International best practices for AI in healthcare
